1 Measuring

1.1 The Empirical Method

All scientific insight begins when a curious and attentive person wonders about some phenomenon, and begins a detailed qualitative observation of this aspect of nature. This observing process then can become more and more quantitative, the object of interest increasingly idealized, until it becomes an experiment asking a well-defined question. The answers to this experiment, the measured data, are organized into tables, and can be graphically visualized in diagram form to facilitate the search for correlations and dependencies. After calculating or estimating the precision of the measurement, the so-called experimental error, one can interpolate and search for a description or at least an approximation in terms of known mathematical curves or formulae. From such empirical connections, conformities to known laws may be discovered. These are mostly formulated in mathematical language (e.g. as differential equations). Once one has found such a connection, one wants to "understand" it. This means either one finds a theory (e.g. some known physical laws) from which one can derive the experimentally obtained data, or one tries using a "hypothesis" to guess the equation which underlies the phenomenon. Obviously also for doing this task a lot of mathematics is necessary. Finally mathematics is needed once again to make predictions which are intended to be checked against experiments, and so on. In such an upward spiral science is progressing.
